## How It Started

A gym opened near my office. I walked in thinking it'd be a one-time thing. Six months later I was there four times a week.

Climbing is unusual because it demands everything at once — finger strength, footwork technique, route-reading, mental composure — and you can't fake your way through any of them.

## The First Month: Humbling

Started on top-rope at the gym. My forearms would pump out after 20 minutes and I'd spend the next hour watching other people and wondering how they made it look effortless.

Key early lessons:
- **Footwork first.** Climbing is mostly legs, not arms. Trust your feet on holds that feel too small.
- **Straight arms on the wall.** Bent arms tire out fast. Hang on your skeleton, not your muscles.
- **You're climbing with your brain.** The physical fitness follows, but reading the route — where to place each foot, how to position your hips — is the actual skill.

## Months 2–4: Building a Base

Started projecting (repeatedly attempting routes at my limit) rather than just doing laps on comfortable grades. This is where real improvement happens.

Supplemented with:
- Dead hangs and finger board work (carefully — finger injuries are common and slow to heal)
- Core training — a strong core means better body tension on the wall
- Watching experienced climbers as much as possible

Got my top-rope certification at the gym, then started learning to belay partners.

## Getting Lead Certified

Lead climbing is where you clip your rope into protection as you go up, rather than the rope being pre-attached from above. A fall means falling twice the distance to your last clip, plus rope stretch. This changes the mental game significantly.

Took the gym's lead course over three sessions. The skills are learnable. The head game — committing to clips above your last protection — takes longer.

Got certified and started leading 5.9s in the gym. Felt like starting from scratch again.

## First Outdoor Climb

Went to a local crag with an experienced friend who knew the area. Outdoor climbing is a different world — the rock is irregular, the grades feel different, and there's no padding if you deck.

The first time I stood at the base of an outdoor route and looked at natural rock instead of a colored plastic hold, I genuinely didn't know how to read it. Spent half the time on my friend's beta (their advice on how to do the route).

By the end of the day I'd led my first outdoor route clean. One of the better feelings I can remember.

## Where I Am Now

Comfortably leading 5.10a-c in the gym, working on 5.10d. Outdoors, a grade or two below that. Still working on footwork precision — it's always the footwork.

If you're thinking about starting, the learning curve is steep and the community at most gyms is genuinely welcoming. Go.
