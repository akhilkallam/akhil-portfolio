## Day 1 — Arrival and Shibuya

Landing at Narita after a 10-hour flight, the first thing that hits you is the organization. Everything works. The trains run on the minute, the signage is clear, and the staff are politely helpful even when you're clearly lost.

Checked into a small hotel in Shinjuku. The room was impossibly tiny but designed with such precision that nothing felt cramped. Headed straight to Shibuya to watch the famous crossing at rush hour. Nothing quite prepares you for the scale of it — thousands of people moving in every direction simultaneously, and yet somehow no collisions.

## Day 2 — Senso-ji and Akihabara

Up at 5am to beat the crowds to Senso-ji. The temple in the early morning mist, with barely a soul around and incense drifting through the air, was one of those moments that makes a trip worth it.

Spent the afternoon wandering Akihabara, which feels like a different planet. Six floors of electronics components, anime merchandise, and retro video games. Picked up some obscure mechanical keyboard switches I'd been hunting for.

## Day 3 — Harajuku, Meiji Shrine, and Dinner in Shimokitazawa

Meiji Shrine before the crowds, then the sharp contrast of Harajuku's Takeshita Street. Found a tiny jazz bar in Shimokitazawa that evening — one of those underground places you only discover by following a handwritten sign down a flight of stairs.

## Day 4 — Day Trip to Nikko

Two hours north by Shinkansen, Nikko's shrine complex is visually overwhelming in the best way. Gold leaf everywhere, cryptomeria trees that are centuries old, and a crowd a fraction the size of Senso-ji's. Worth every minute of the commute.

## Day 5 — Tsukiji and Slow Exploration

Final morning at Tsukiji outer market for the freshest tuna I've ever eaten at 7am. Spent the rest of the day walking without a plan — the best way to see Tokyo. Ended up in a tiny gallery in Yanaka and a record shop in Shimokitazawa that had no business being as good as it was.

---

Tokyo rewards preparation but it also rewards wandering. The city operates at a pace and precision that's humbling, and its depth — a new layer of history or subculture around every corner — means you could come back ten times and still feel like a first-timer.
